Facebook’s latest hollow effort to fight climate change denial

Facebook prefers to counter false information with more information. It doesn't work.

Kate Cox - Sep 16, 2020 3:06 pm UTC

reader comments

Facebook this week implemented yet another new initiative meant to combat rampant, dangerous disinformation on the platform—this time, relating to the climate crisis. Unfortunately this initiative, like countless others before it, seems likely to generate positive headlines for about a week before disappearing unremarked into obscurity, solving exactly zero of Facebook's deeper problems along the way.

"One of the biggest lessons we have learned from the COVID-19 pandemic is how powerful Facebook can be for connecting people to accurate, expert advice and information during a global crisis," Facebook wrote in a corporate blog post. To that end, Facebook is launching a new "Climate Change Information Center" module and landing page that will, in theory, connect users to up-to-date, fact-based information grounded in reality.

Further Reading

Sweeping internal Facebook memo: “I have blood on my hands”

Users in the US and a small handful of other countries may already have seen a notification about the new climate information center appear in their newsfeeds this week, either on desktop or on mobile. The green box implores you by name to "see how our climate is changing."

Clicking through takes you directly to the climate science information center, featuring a handful of climate facts, links to a selection of vetted organizations, graphs of temperature facts customized to your location, and a curated selection of relevant news stories from major outlets such as USA Today, Vox, The New York Times, BuzzFeed, and CNN. Users can also choose to follow or like any other page, group, or individual on the platform.

Facebook said it modeled the climate information center on its COVID-19 information landing page, which it launched this spring after coronavirus misinformation and falsehoods were already widespread across social media platforms. About 600 million users have at one point or another clicked through to view the COVID-19 information module, the company said—about 22 percent of Facebook's 2.7 billion monthly active users.

We’ve been here before

Lies, falsehoods, and disinformation are not a new problem for Facebook—and the company unfortunately has a spotty track record when it comes to handling misinformation of all types.

Company CEO Mark Zuckerberg has long been adamant that his preferred solution for countering bad stuff on Facebook is to have more stuff on Facebook. Rather than banning most forms of falsehood as a policy, Facebook at best labels them with a link to a fact check and encourages users to post their own, ideally more accurate information to counter untruths.

Further Reading

Political ads can lie if they want, Facebook confirms

Sure, the theory goes, some people (including sitting politicians) may use Facebook to be cranks, but it's incumbent on everyone else to use Facebook to say things that are true and hope that the truths can counteract the lies.

At this point, however, it seems clear that policy is not working out as well as Zuckerberg may have hoped. People who spread falsehoods, it turns out, tend not to be operating in good faith, and now Facebook faces even more criticism from all sides.

The spread of false information on Facebook can have dire real-world effects. The impact of Russia's use of social media to manipulate the 2016 US presidential election is by now well known. In 2018, the company admitted that false content posted by Myanmar's military was directly contributing to the genocide of the Rohingya ethnic minority in that nation. As recently as this week, a former data scientist for Facebook wrote a damning memo describing several instances of inauthentic political behavior on Facebook fanning flames worldwide during her two years with the company.

Nor are inauthentic actors the only problem Facebook faces. Plenty of people and organizations who spread false information are extremely clear about who they are and what they're doing. Anti-vaccination groups, for example, spread misinformation far and wide. Facebook prohibits anti-vax groups from posting advertisements on the platform, but organic content still generates engagement, even though Facebook applies fact-checking labels. Now, Facebook even finds itself facing a lawsuit from anti-vaxxers who don't like being fact-checked.

Finding the Information desk

Facebook's fact-check labels for major topics such as election security, COVID-19, and now climate change lead to their information centers. The topic-centered landing pages do in fact direct users to good-quality, reliable, local, and national information on the topics at hand, as they are designed to.

These centers, however, rapidly end up buried in a sea of other Facebook content you may or may not ever use or, in fact, realize exists. This image of Facebook's mobile menu, for example, was assembled on September 15:

With the election coming up in fewer than 50 days—and early voting beginning in my state this Friday—it's good to see that Facebook's vaunted election information hub is all the way down at the bottom of the list, beneath nearly two dozen other features I have literally never used.

The climate climate

The climate change information center is facing another steep challenge entirely of Facebook's own making: the company's apparent unwillingness to anger powerful, popular groups and individuals that share false information about climate change.

Further Reading

Facebook overrides fact-checks when climate science is “opinion”

Facebook's stance on climate change is that most denial is a matter of opinion and therefore not subject to fact-checking.

"When someone posts content based on false facts—even if it’s an op-ed or editorial—it is still eligible for fact-checking," Facebook Communications Director Andy Stone told The New York Times in July. "We're working to make this clearer in our guidelines so our fact checkers can use their judgment to determine whether it is an attempt to mask false information under the guise of opinion."

However, Facebook has at least twice overturned the rulings of fact-checking experts who did review climate-related posts and found them to be partly or entirely false. Climate Feedback, a group that partners with Facebook as one of its fact-checkers, marked a 2019 Washington Examiner op-ed as false. A climate-change-denial organization, the CO2 Coalition, complained to Facebook about the fact-check, and Facebook removed the label on the post.

Earlier this year, an article about climate change published by The Daily Wire, a conservative site that is also Facebook's biggest publisher, received a "partly false" rating from Climate Feedback. The newsletter Popular Information obtained internal Facebook documents showing that Facebook staff agreed with the "partly false" rating, but Facebook withdrew the label anyway after the author of the Daily Wire article complained about being "censored."

In short: fact-checking is not enough—especially if Facebook is just going to walk it back whenever someone complains.

A coalition of media and environment watchdog groups—including the Sierra Club, Greenpeace, and the Union of Concerned Scientists—issued a joint statement giving Facebook half credit for their actions.

"This new policy is a small step forward but does not address the larger climate disinformation crisis hiding in plain sight," the groups wrote. "Climate deniers are an easy group to define—we gave Facebook the list. As we’ve seen with the fires in Facebook’s backyard, active hurricane season, and extreme weather, the dangers of climate change are urgent, real, and deadly. Just as Facebook has taken responsibility for its own carbon emissions, it must take responsibility to stop climate deniers from spreading disinformation on its platform."
